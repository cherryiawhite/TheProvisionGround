The Provision GroundPrefatory Note
Prefatory note

On the title, and on the aim

The provision ground was the plot that enslaved people worked for themselves at the edge of the plantation. On it they grew food, saved seed, kept knowledge, and practiced a form of self direction the plantation was built to deny. It is the oldest site of Black ecological self determination in the Atlantic world, and it is the figure this program takes for its name.

The name is deliberate. It signals that the climate question pursued here is not only a question of harm and exposure but of knowledge, labor, and refusal, of what Black and Indigenous Atlantic communities have grown, kept, and built under precisely the conditions of duress that the climate horizon now generalizes. It also locates this work within a scholarly lineage the CCDGB Lab already commands, including the study of Caribbean peasantries and the post Creole imagination, while turning that lineage toward an environmental frontier.

This document is a prospectus, not a summary. It takes the lab's published theoretical architecture as given and asks a forward looking question: what might a sustained inquiry at the intersection of climate, decoloniality, and global Blackness investigate, what evidence would it require, and what would it publish. The brainstorm that preceded it proposed themes and questions. This prospectus proposes a frontier.

How to read this prospectus

Its center of gravity is Part II, a set of research provocations whose answers are not yet settled. Parts III through V supply the apparatus those provocations require: a structured agenda, the methods and data infrastructure, and a publication architecture in which every line of inquiry terminates in a discrete and citable scholarly output. Spatial and computational methods appear throughout, but as instruments in service of the questions rather than as the questions themselves.

The program is designed to cohere as a contribution the lab can carry into the CHCI Climate Futures initiative and well beyond it, toward a special issue, an edited volume, a body of peer reviewed scholarship, and a public archive. Every claim in what follows is offered provisionally and in good faith, and is open to correction.

Respectfully submitted,

Part I · OrientationThe Provision Ground
IOrientation

The wager, and the publics it must reach

The program proceeds from a single wager, which the lab's scholarship has already established and which this prospectus does not restate so much as operationalize.

The wager is that the climate crisis cannot be adequately understood, much less addressed, as a purely scientific or technical matter. It is the long consequence of a world organized for extraction, of land, of labor, of water, and of breath. The theoretical optic the lab supplies, in which global Blackness is read as fugitive, fungible, and spectral, in which the break names both the rupture of colonial modernity and the opening it leaves, and in which decoloniality designates the refusal of that modernity's terms, does explanatory work that mainstream environmental discourse cannot.

Placed beside the climate question, this optic accounts for why the geography of harm assumes the shape it does, why flood, petrochemical corridor, and eroding shoreline so reliably find the same communities. It treats the plantation not as a closed historical chapter but as an operating infrastructure that still organizes soil, drainage, coastline, and settlement. And it locates, in the very communities that order dispossessed, the ecological knowledge and imaginative capacity that any livable future will require.

What this program adds is neither a restatement of the theory nor a single technique, but a sustained inquiry: a set of questions worth a multiyear program, the evidence and methods adequate to them, and the scholarly forms in which their answers can travel. The provision ground is the program's emblem because it holds both halves of the wager at once, the violence of the plantation and the self direction practiced at its edge.

The sections that follow assume this orientation and do not argue it again. They turn instead to what is not yet known.

Three publics, and the obligation to reach all three

The scholarly field

Peer review, the CHCI network, and journals such as Cultural Dynamics. This public requires rigor, citation, and a demonstrable contribution to debates on decoloniality, the environmental humanities, and the archive.

The public sphere

Policy audiences, cultural institutions, and general readers. This public requires the argument made legible and consequential, in essays, exhibitions, and accessible scholarship.

The communities

The people whose knowledge constitutes the evidence. This public requires that the work be answerable to them and returned to them, as authorship and not extraction.

The methodological commitment of the program is that a single body of carefully gathered evidence can address all three publics at once, that a finding rigorous enough for a journal can also be legible to a policymaker and accountable to a community. This is difficult, and the difficulty is the point. It is also, as Part V argues, the basis on which the program becomes genuinely publishable rather than merely productive.

The substrate and what grows from it

Six lenses, one stratigraphy

The six organizing lenses that the preceding brainstorm proposed are not six separate projects. They are six readings of a single terrain, and they are best understood as strata. The plantation is the substrate; everything else accumulates, moves, survives, and imagines upon it. The provocations of Part II then drill down through these strata to reach questions that none of the lenses, on its own, can answer.

Read from the bottom upward. The plantation is the ground on which the other strata rest, and the provision ground is the seam of self direction running through all of them.

The value of the stratigraphic model is that it gives the program a spine rather than a scatter, and a single sentence any collaborator can carry: the program studies what the plantation built, what survived it, and what is being grown in its place. Each provocation that follows can be located against these strata, and each scholarly output can declare which stratum it works in, so that the parts continue to sum to a whole.

IIResearch Provocations

Eight questions at the frontier

What follows is the substance of the program. Each provocation poses a question whose answer is not presently known, to this author or, so far as the available scholarship indicates, to the field. They are offered as the frontier the program would work toward, not as conclusions it would illustrate.

Several conventions govern this section. Each provocation states a frame drawn from the lab's own conceptual vocabulary, then the open questions it raises, then the stakes of answering them, and finally the scholarly forms in which an answer could be published. The questions are written to be genuinely answerable within a sustained program, yet genuinely uncertain in their outcome, which is the condition of original research. Where a provocation could resolve in more than one direction, that openness is named rather than concealed.

The eight are not of equal scale. The first four are treated at length, because each opens an independent line of inquiry capable of anchoring articles, datasets, and collaborations on its own. The remaining four are stated more briefly, as near adjacent frontiers that the same evidence base would render tractable. A closing note gathers several further questions that the program would hold in reserve.

Part II · Research ProvocationsThe Provision Ground
IProvocation

The Provision Ground as Climate Science

Within the plantation, enslaved people cultivated the provision ground, the marginal plot on which they grew their own food. This program asks whether that ground, and its diasporic kin, constitutes an unrecognized body of climate adaptive knowledge.

The frame. The lab's scholarship on Caribbean peasantries already treats the provision ground as a site of incipient autonomy. Set beside the climate question, it becomes something further. The provision ground and its relations, the Gullah yard garden, the Brazilian roça, the Garifuna and Caribbean conuco, were refined under exactly the conditions, scarcity, heat, salt, flood, and soil exhaustion, that now define the climate horizon for the wider world. Polyculture, intercropping, seed saving, the selection of flood and drought tolerant cultivars, and water and shade management were practiced there not as heritage but as survival science.

The open questions

Can this agronomic knowledge be reconstructed with enough specificity, from oral history, surviving practice, herbaria, and colonial records, to be tested against contemporary adaptation science? Where it diverges from the prevailing orthodoxy of climate smart agriculture, which is the sounder guide? Does the provision ground preserve cultivars or techniques that institutional science has overlooked, and if so, what follows for how adaptation is funded and designed?

These are not rhetorical questions. The author does not know whether the reconstruction is feasible at the needed resolution, nor whether the practices would outperform current recommendations, nor where they would fail. That uncertainty is the reason the inquiry is worth a program rather than an essay.

Why it breaks ground

It reframes Black ecological knowledge from cultural heritage to testable expertise, and it places the humanities in a position to revise, not merely to contextualize, a scientific and policy field. It also gives the program its title and its moral center, the ground of self direction rather than the ground of harm.

What it would publish

A peer reviewed article positioned across environmental humanities and agrarian studies; a curated and citable register of cultivars and practices with documented provenance, suitable for a data journal; and a field guide that returns the knowledge to the communities that hold it.

Part II · Research ProvocationsThe Provision Ground
IIProvocation

Fungibility and the Price of Climate

The lab reads global Blackness as fungible, rendered exchangeable and convertible into value. This program asks whether the same logic now organizes the financial machinery of climate response.

The frame. Fungibility is among the most precise of the lab's concepts: it names the rendering of Black life as interchangeable, abstractable, priceable. The contemporary apparatus of climate finance trades in abstractions of a strikingly similar form. Carbon offset markets render a hectare of forest or a community's land exchangeable against a distant corporation's emissions. Catastrophe bonds and parametric insurance price and trade the risk of disaster. A growing field of resilience finance converts vulnerability itself into an asset class. In each case a living relation to land is made into a tradable quantity.

The open questions

Is the carbon offset a contemporary technology of fungibility, and does its burden fall, as before, disproportionately on Black and Indigenous land? What happens to a community's relation to place when that place becomes a climate abstraction owned and traded elsewhere? And can fungibility, a concept developed within Black critical theory, perform analytic work on these instruments that conventional political economy does not?

Whether the analogy holds under scrutiny is genuinely uncertain. It may be that offset markets differ from chattel fungibility in ways that matter more than their resemblance, and the program would be obligated to find that out rather than to assume it. The value of the question lies precisely in its being open.

Why it breaks ground

It builds an original bridge between Black studies and the financialization of climate, a juncture that neither literature currently occupies. It carries the lab's decolonial argument directly into the domain of policy and capital, where climate decisions are in fact being made.

What it would publish

A theoretical article positioned for a leading journal in critical theory or environmental humanities; a critical lexicon of climate finance instruments read through fungibility; and a public facing essay translating the argument for a policy readership.

IIIProvocation

The Spectral and the Sensor

The lab's figure of the spectral names a presence that haunts, felt but not captured. Set against the apparatus of environmental measurement, it becomes a method for reading what the instruments cannot see.

The frame. The contemporary environment is known largely through measurement: satellites, sensor networks, and screening tools such as the federal environmental justice indices. Every regime of measurement, however, produces its own structured blindness. Monitoring stations are unevenly placed. Records are kept for some harms and not others. Certain knowledges were never inscribed because those who held them were never asked. The spectral, in this setting, ceases to be only a literary figure and becomes a research instrument trained on the negative space of the data.

The open questions

Where are the environmental data deserts, and do they coincide with historically Black and Indigenous geographies, such that the communities most exposed are also the least measured? What would it mean to treat absence, the unmeasured and the unrecorded and the haunting, as itself a form of evidence rather than a mere gap? And can a disciplined practice of counter data, of reading what the sensor fails to register, be built and defended as method?

The program does not assume that the gaps will align neatly with the geography of dispossession, though there is reason to suspect they often do. Establishing where they do and do not, and why, is itself a finding. This provocation also disciplines the program's own use of measurement, since it begins from the premise that data is never neutral or complete.

Why it breaks ground

It converts a theoretical figure into a method, and it contributes to the emerging field of data justice a distinctively decolonial account of absence. It also models intellectual honesty about the limits of the program's own instruments.

What it would publish

A methodological article; a digital scholarly object that documents the geography of the unmeasured, a map of what is not measured rather than of what is; and a reusable dataset of identified data deserts with their archival correlates.

IVProvocation

Creole Ecologies

The post Creole imagination treats creolization, the unplanned emergence of new forms from forced encounter, as a generative cultural logic. This program asks whether creolization is also an ecological logic.

The frame. Creolization theory describes how the new arises from coerced mixture, improvisation under constraint, and recombination rather than restoration of an origin. Atlantic landscapes shaped by the plantation are themselves creolized in just this sense. The ruined estate, the dredged and rerouted estuary, the species introduced and naturalized across centuries, the ruderal growth that colonizes contaminated ground: these are neither pristine nor simply degraded but recombinant, carrying the signature of empire in their very composition. The dominant restoration paradigm, by contrast, often longs for a baseline before contact that no longer exists and perhaps never did.

The open questions

Can creolization theory describe how damaged Atlantic ecologies actually recover and recombine, more truthfully than a restoration ethic organized around a lost origin? What is owed to novel ecosystems that bear the mark of slavery and empire, and on what grounds? Does a creole ecology change what conservation and restoration should aim at in landscapes that were never innocent of history?

It is far from settled that a cultural theory can be carried responsibly into ecology, and the program would have to test the translation against the science rather than assert it. The reward, if the translation holds, is a genuinely new way of valuing the landscapes the plantation left behind.

Why it breaks ground

It joins Caribbean theory to live debates in restoration ecology and the study of novel ecosystems, a pairing that is, to this author's knowledge, unmade. It is the kind of question that can only be pursued across the humanities and the sciences together.

What it would publish

An article co authorable with an ecologist and positioned for an interdisciplinary venue; a field and photographic essay on creolized landscapes; and a contribution to the ethics of restoration.

Part II · Research ProvocationsThe Provision Ground
Near adjacent frontiers

Four further questions the same evidence base would open

The provocations below are stated more briefly. Each is independently publishable, and each draws on the methods and the fieldwork the first four already require, so that the program gains them at a low marginal cost.

V

Thermal Afterlives

Heat as the connective tissue from the plantation labor regime to the present.

The disciplining of the laboring body under heat was central to plantation production. Extreme heat now falls most heavily on Black agricultural, outdoor, and incarcerated workers. The open question: can a continuous history of thermal inequality be written, one that links the plantation's management of heat and labor to today's distribution of heat death, and would that history change how heat policy is framed? It would publish as an article across labor history and public health, accompanied by an oral history of working in heat.

VI

Hydro Archives

Specific waters read at once as documents of violence and as kin.

Tidal creeks, blackwater swamps, aquifers, and the Atlantic itself hold both the sediment of historical violence and, within Black and Indigenous cosmologies, the status of relations rather than resources. The open question: can a watershed be read as a historical document, and what does sound, the recorded voice of a water body and its surround, register that a map cannot? It would publish as a hydro archive and a series of sonic essays, with an accompanying article on water as method.

Provocations V and VI share a commitment that runs through the whole program: that evidence is not confined to what can be plotted on a coordinate plane. The body in heat and the voice of a river are data of a different order, and the methods of Part IV are built to gather them.

Near adjacent frontiers, continued

From repair to time

VII

A Reparations Ecology

Community led repair as a coherent paradigm, distinct from carbon accounting.

Across the Atlantic, communities already practice forms of environmental repair that the dominant climate vocabulary does not recognize as such: land return, Gullah and Garifuna restoration of marsh and mangrove, Quilombo land titling, the rebuilding of provision grounds. The open question: can these be theorized together as a reparations ecology, a paradigm of repair designed from the sovereignty tradition rather than from emissions arithmetic, and what would such repair require of states and institutions? It would publish as a framework article and a case archive, with a policy facing paper for a wider readership.

VIII

Chronotopes of the Break

Plantation time, climate time, and diasporic time, in collision.

Three temporalities meet in this work and do not obviously agree: plantation time, the cyclical time of extraction; climate time, the slow violence and deep time and abrupt tipping points of the earth system; and diasporic time, the wake, the co presence of ancestor and descendant, the Afrofuturist future folded into the present. The open question: do these times align, or interfere, and what follows for how a community's climate future can truthfully be narrated? It would publish as a theoretical article on temporality, the program's most purely conceptual contribution.

Further frontiers, held in reserve

Several additional questions sit within reach of the same program and are recorded here so that they are not lost. Each could be activated as the evidence accumulates.

  • Managed retreat as second displacement. When the state relocates a coastal community, does it repeat the structure of forced removal, and how do communities that have already survived displacement understand and contest the new one?
  • The soil as witness. Can soil science, the lead, the residues, the stratigraphy of a contaminated lot, be read alongside testimony as a material archive of slow violence?
  • Fugitive cartography. Is there a way to map that honors opacity, that withholds and marks refusal rather than claiming total knowledge, and what would such a practice contribute to critical mapping? This question is taken up methodologically in Part IV.

How the eight cohere

One program, not eight projects

The provocations are distinct, but they are not unrelated. Read against the stratigraphy of Part I, they distribute across the strata and bind them, and read against one another they form a single argument: that the plantation order produced the climate crisis, that its logics persist in the present in measurable and contestable forms, and that the communities it dispossessed hold knowledge and practices of repair the future will require.

This division is heuristic, not rigid. Provocation III disciplines the whole program's relation to evidence; Provocation VIII frames its sense of time; the provision ground of Provocation I gives the program its name and its ethical orientation. Taken together they are designed to be more than the sum of their articles, which is the condition under which the work becomes a special issue and an edited volume rather than a scattering of papers, as Part V proposes.

IIIFrom Provocations to Projects

An operational agenda

A frontier becomes a fundable program only when its questions are translated into projects with a defined method, an evidence base, and a publication. This section performs that translation and sequences the result.

The translation does not collapse the provocations into a checklist. It assigns to each a primary scholarly form and a realistic position on two axes a funder weighs: how achievable the work is within a two year window, and how far its results will travel. Plotting the projects honestly, rather than presenting them all as equally ready, is itself a signal of judgment. The visionary projects are named as the program's horizon; the tractable ones are named as its point of departure.

Placement is the author's judgment and is meant to start a conversation, not to settle one.

Part III · From Provocations to ProjectsThe Provision Ground
The anchor project in detail

Project I, fully specified

The provision ground study is specified here as a worked example of how each project in the agenda would be built, from question to evidence to output.

The design

The study reconstructs the agronomy of provision grounds at the program's anchor sites through a triangulated evidence base: oral histories with elders and growers who carry the practice; documentation of surviving gardens and their cultivars; consultation of herbaria and seed collections; and colonial and estate records that register what was grown at the margins. The reconstructed practices are then set in dialogue with contemporary adaptation science, identifying convergences, divergences, and overlooked techniques.

The standard of evidence

Every claim is anchored to a documented source, and the testimony is treated as primary expertise rather than as illustration. Where the historical record and living memory disagree, the disagreement is preserved and analyzed, not resolved away.

Outputs, in order
  • A peer reviewed article on provision ground agronomy as adaptation knowledge.
  • A citable cultivar and practice register, published as a data paper with full provenance.
  • A field guide returned to the participating communities.
  • A public essay translating the findings for a general readership.

Why it anchors the program

It is the project most likely to yield both a rigorous publication and a tangible good for the communities it studies, and it demonstrates the program's central claim, that Black ecological knowledge is expertise the climate field needs, in the most concrete possible form.

What remains genuinely uncertain

Whether the reconstruction can reach the resolution the comparison requires, and whether the practices will prove superior, comparable, or inferior to current recommendations, cannot be known in advance. The program commits to reporting whichever result the evidence supports. That commitment is what distinguishes a research program from an argument seeking confirmation.

Part IV · Methods and DataThe Provision Ground
IVMethods and Data

An evidence base, and how it is gathered

The provocations of Part II are answerable only on the strength of the evidence brought to them. The program is humanities led in its methods, and it treats spatial and computational tools as instruments in service of interpretation rather than as the source of its claims.

This ordering is deliberate. The questions at the program's frontier are questions of meaning, history, and power, and they are pursued first through the methods proper to such questions. The atlas and the computational apparatus that follow in this part are valuable precisely because they are disciplined by, and answerable to, the interpretive work described here.

The core methods

Archival and historical research

Colonial and estate records, cadastral and land use documents, parish and plantation registers, and the slave trade record, read critically and against their grain for what they register and what they suppress.

Oral history and ethnography

Sustained, consent governed interviews and participant observation that treat community knowledge as primary expertise. This is the program's central evidentiary commitment and the source the other methods serve.

Field and botanical survey

Documentation of surviving gardens, cultivars, waters, and landscapes, conducted with the communities and feeding Projects I, IV, and VI directly.

Close reading and theory

The interpretive work, on finance, on temporality, on creolization, that turns evidence into argument and locates the program's claims within the scholarly field.

The ethic that governs collection

All evidence drawn from communities is gathered under a consent and care protocol that establishes ownership, the right to review and to edit, and the return of the material to its source. The camera and the recorder are instruments of witness, not of extraction, and the archive is built to be answerable to the people whose knowledge it holds. This is not an addendum to the method. It is the method.

One instrument among several

The spatial layer, and its limits

Geospatial synthesis is one of the program's instruments, and a powerful one, but it is not its center. A map can make a relationship between history and present harm visible at a glance, and it can hold testimony, document, and datum at a single coordinate. It cannot, on its own, interpret what it shows, and it carries the colonial map's old temptation to claim a total and settled knowledge. The program uses the spatial layer for what it does well and constrains it where it misleads.

A schematic of the program's geography, anchor sites within the wider Atlantic it documents. The atlas built on this is one synthesis instrument, not the program's thesis.

A critical commitment: fugitive cartography

Informed by Provocation III and by the right to opacity in Caribbean thought, the program maps in a way that can withhold. It marks refusal, declines to plot what a community asks to keep unplotted, and shows where its own knowledge ends. The map is thus made an object of inquiry as much as an instrument of it.

Part IV · Methods and DataThe Provision Ground
Collecting and structuring the evidence

A data model built for interpretation

Evidence gathered across eight projects and several sites must be structured so that it can be queried, compared, and published, without flattening the testimony into a statistic. The program adopts a record that holds three registers together at every unit of evidence, whether that unit is a site, a cultivar, an interview, or a water body.

Structuring the evidence relationally, rather than as isolated cases, is what allows the program to ask questions across sites and across centuries: which practices recur where, which knowledges travel along which passages of the break, where a documented harm meets a withheld silence. It is also what makes the underlying corpus publishable in its own right, as the data papers of Part V propose. The model is designed so that the act of organizing the evidence is already an act of interpretation, and so that the community's testimony is never subordinated to the measurement that surrounds it.

Part IV · Methods and DataThe Provision Ground
Showing the evidence

Visualization beyond the map

Not all evidence belongs on a coordinate plane, and a program that reduced everything to the map would mistake one instrument for the whole toolkit. The program collects and presents its findings in several registers, each chosen for what it shows that the others cannot.

Relational and network views

Graphs of how practices, cultivars, and knowledges connect across sites, making visible the kinship of the diaspora's ecological knowledge rather than its mere location. This is how Provocation I's shared agronomy and Provocation VI's named waters become legible as a system.

Sonic presentation

Field recordings, oral testimony, and the soundscape of a place, the refinery hum, the lost birdsong, the sea, composed into sonic essays. Sound carries presence, grief, and the body in ways the visual cannot, and it serves Provocation VI directly.

The visual essay

Photography and document in deliberate sequence, pairing the archival image of a landscape with its present condition. An argument made by juxtaposition, suited to Projects I and IV.

The data paper

The structured corpus itself, documented and released as a citable scholarly object so that others can build on it. Visualization here is the careful presentation of provenance and structure, treated in Part V.

The principle

The form of presentation is chosen to fit the evidence and the question, never the reverse. A finding about heat and the body may be best carried by an oral history; a finding about shared agronomy by a network graph; a finding about a watershed by sound. Method follows the question, and the question follows the community's knowledge.

Part IV · Methods and DataThe Provision Ground
A bounded instrument

Computation, used sparingly and accountably

Computational methods have a defined and limited place in this program. They scale the labor of listening and reading; they do not produce its knowledge.

Used with restraint, computation lets a small team work across a large corpus: transcribing and translating hours of oral history into searchable text, reading colonial records at scale to extract names and dates for the historical layer, and surfacing patterns across distant communities for a human to examine or to reject. These are real efficiencies, and they serve several projects. They are also, in this program, kept firmly in a supporting role, subordinate to the interpretive and community accountable methods that precede them in this part.

The commitments that govern its use

  • It augments, it does not replace. The machine proposes patterns; the community and the scholar decide what they mean.
  • It is accountable to communities. Nothing derived from a community's testimony is published before that community has reviewed it.
  • It is transparent. Each computational step is documented so that a result can be reproduced and examined, as scholarship requires.
  • It never fabricates. It does not generate words and attribute them to a person, and any synthetic image is labeled as such.

These commitments are not a disclaimer appended to the method; they are what makes the method admissible in a program whose entire argument concerns whose knowledge counts. Stated plainly, computation here is a magnifying glass held over community knowledge, never a substitute for it. The author's facility with these tools is offered as a means of carrying the program's labor efficiently, not as the program's distinguishing idea.

The question is never what the instrument can do. It is what the evidence requires, and what the people whose knowledge it is would have it do. The rule for every instrument in this part

Part V · A Publication ArchitectureThe Provision Ground
A second order of output

The evidence itself, published

A distinctive feature of this program is that its evidence base is designed to be published as scholarship in its own right, not merely consumed privately to produce articles. This serves the decolonial commitment to an open and accountable archive, and it multiplies the program's scholarly footprint.

Data papers and datasets

The cultivar and practice register of Project I, the corpus of identified data deserts of Project III, and the structured oral history collection are each documented and released through a data journal or repository, with full provenance and clear terms of use. A data paper is peer reviewed, citable, and credits the labor of building the evidence, labor that conventional publishing renders invisible.

A digital scholarly edition

The atlas and the structured corpus, taken together, constitute a work of digital scholarship that can be submitted for peer review as such, so that the platform is cited as research rather than dismissed as outreach. Several venues now review digital editions to the same standard as monographs.

Why this matters for the program

Publishing the evidence does three things at once. It honors the communities by making the archive open and answerable. It lets other scholars build on the work, which is how a program becomes a field. And it produces durable, citable outputs in the first year, before the major articles are complete, keeping the program's contribution visible from the start.

A standard to meet

All released data follows established principles for findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable scholarship, adapted to the program's consent commitments so that nothing is opened that a community has asked to keep closed.

A note on sequencing

Because data papers can be completed before their companion articles, they are the program's earliest formal publications. They establish a citable record in year one, demonstrate the evidence base to reviewers of the larger work, and give the communities a tangible return long before the scholarship concludes.

Part V · A Publication ArchitectureThe Provision Ground
The third public

Public scholarship and the return to communities

A program answerable to the communities whose knowledge it studies cannot treat publication as a purely academic act. Its outputs reach the public sphere and the communities themselves with the same seriousness they bring to the journal, and in a defined order that places the communities first.

The return, as method and as ethic

Before any academic premiere, findings go home: community gatherings and story circles at each site, where people see the work, correct the record, and extend it. This is not outreach appended to the research. It is how the evidence stays accurate and how the archive keeps the trust on which it depends. A finding a community rejects is a finding the program reexamines.

The public forms

  • Essays for a wide readership that translate the program's arguments without diluting them.
  • A digital scholarly edition, open and free, that makes the archive navigable to anyone.
  • An exhibition that gathers the program's photographic, documentary, and testimonial work into a public encounter.
The exhibition as a culminating form

An exhibition does what neither the article nor the dataset can. It returns the work to a public in a form that is felt as well as understood, it travels between the community and the cultural institution, and it converts the program's scholarship into a public event that funders and audiences recognize. It is the natural home for the visual essays of Projects I and IV and the sonic work of Project VI.

One standard, three publics

The discipline of the program is that the same finding is rendered for the journal, the public, and the community without contradiction. What persuades a reviewer must also be legible to a reader and answerable to a community. Holding all three is the program's signature difficulty, and its signature contribution.

The return is not the last step of the research. It is the test the research must pass, and the relationship without which there would be no archive to publish at all. The dissemination ethic of the program

Part VI · Teaching the ProgramThe Provision Ground
VITeaching the Program

A seminar built from the research

Pedagogy is the most durable form of impact the program can produce. A graduate seminar converts the research into something that outlives the grant, trains a cohort to extend it, and turns the program's evidence into a teachable archive.

Proposed graduate seminar

The Plantation and the Planet

A seminar in the environmental humanities of the Black Atlantic, in which students learn to read climate vulnerability as colonial history and contribute to one of the program's research projects directly.

Learning outcomes

  • Account for how plantation geography became climate infrastructure, with cases.
  • Read a dataset, a map, or a record as an argument rather than as neutral information.
  • Conduct a consent governed oral history interview and situate it in context.
  • Produce one documented, community accountable unit of evidence to the program's standard.
  • Locate the work within decolonial theory: the break, the wake, fungibility, creolization.

The arc, in five movements

  1. Ground. The plantation as infrastructure; the provision ground as counterpoint.
  2. Movement. Forced migration and the climate diaspora.
  3. Accumulation. Slow violence, toxicity, and the unmeasured.
  4. Memory. The body and the watershed as archive; oral history as method.
  5. Otherwise. Creole ecologies and the climate imagination.

The signature assignment

Each student adopts one unit of evidence, a site, a cultivar, a water body, or a testimony, and documents it to the program's standard across the three registers. By the term's end the cohort has collectively extended the program's archive. The research feeds the teaching and the teaching feeds the research, a loop that is itself a fundable and publishable story.

Coordination and roles

A division of labor

For the program to move efficiently, the division of labor should be explicit. What follows states it plainly, with the lines held clearly on both sides.

Carried by the author

  • Production and build. The spatial layer, the data infrastructure, the digital edition, the visual deliverables.
  • Evidence gathering. Fieldwork, documentation, interviewing, and the structuring of the archive.
  • First drafts and figures. So that scholarship reaches the senior scholar as material to react to.
  • Computational support. Bounded and accountable, as set out in Part IV.
  • Coordination. Timelines, deliverable tracking, and the cadence of outputs.

Held by the senior scholar

  • The theoretical framework and its claims. The author serves the framework rather than steering it.
  • The scholarly voice and the final word on every argument that bears the program's name.
  • The relationships and the trust built across the field over decades.
  • Authorship and credit, which remain entirely the senior scholar's to decide.
In short

The author is most useful as the person who turns open questions into evidence and drafts and built objects, so that the senior scholar's attention is reserved for the interpretive work only she can do.

What would make the collaboration efficient

A standing check in at a regular interval, a clear first priority among the projects of Part III, and the latitude to bring work forward early and in draft. The author works best given a direction and the freedom to pursue it.

Practically, the author is available immediately and locally, including for the near term sprint, and can sustain the program's momentum with even a modest weekly commitment. An invoice for work completed to date is provided separately.

Appendix B · Working GlossaryThe Provision Ground
+Appendix B

A working glossary

Working definitions for the terms this program leans on, so that any collaborator or reviewer reads them consistently. These are for shared use, not the last word.

The provision ground

The subsistence plot enslaved people worked for themselves at the plantation's edge, a site of food, seed, knowledge, and self direction. The program's emblem.

The break

The rupture of colonial modernity, and the opening it leaves where other futures become thinkable. The program works in the break.

Global Blackness: fugitive, fungible, spectral

Blackness read as a condition and an optic that exceeds the categories colonialism built for it: always escaping control, rendered exchangeable, and haunting the present.

Fungibility

The rendering of life or land as interchangeable and priceable. Provocation II asks whether climate finance revives the logic.

Creolization and creole ecology

The emergence of new forms from forced encounter, extended in Provocation IV from culture to the recombinant ecologies of plantation landscapes.

Counter data

A method of treating absence and the unmeasured as evidence, developed in Provocation III.

The plantation as infrastructure

The plantation treated as a still operating technology that organized land, water, and air, not a closed historical chapter.

Slow violence

After Rob Nixon: harm that is gradual and dispersed, invisible to the logic of spectacle.

The wake

After Christina Sharpe: living in the long aftermath of slavery, and the work of care and consciousness it demands.

Poethics

An ethics inseparable from making and imagining, where how a thing is created carries its moral weight.

Chronotope

A configuration of time and space. Provocation VIII reads plantation, climate, and diasporic time against one another.

Fugitive cartography

A practice of mapping that honors opacity, withholding what a community asks to keep unplotted and marking where its own knowledge ends.

Community accountable

Answerable to the people the work documents: they author, correct, and receive it. The opposite of extraction.
