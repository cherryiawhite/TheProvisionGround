Ground Truth

What a scholar of the Black Atlantic who is also an ecologist,
a diver, and a conservationist brings to the program

In remote sensing and field ecology, ground truth is the practice of leaving the satellite image and going to the site to verify what the data only claims. The Provision Ground asks questions that sit exactly on the seam between the humanities and the environmental sciences, and answering them will require someone who can do both the theory and the ground truth. That person is rare, because the seam is usually assembled from two specialists who do not share a language. Here it is held in one.

The scholar proposing this program is trained in African and African American studies and the theory of the Black Atlantic, and is also trained in ecology and marine science, certified to dive, and experienced in environmental science, policy, and conservation. The two halves are not a coincidence of interests. They are the precise pairing the program's hardest questions demand, and their convergence is itself an asset worth naming plainly to a reviewer.

Why the convergence is the point

A humanities program that needs science must hire it, wait on it, and translate across it. A science program that needs the humanities rarely tries. This program begins with both already fluent in one person, which lowers its cost, shortens its timelines, and lets it ask questions neither field could ask alone. The pages that follow show where that pays off, question by question.

Five advantages the combination creates

Beyond the individual questions, the convergence produces strategic advantages for the program as a whole, several of which a reviewer or a funder will value directly.

01 · Two funding worlds

A humanities led project that a marine scientist can also use is fundable from two directions at once: the humanities and arts programs that anchor it, and the science, ocean, and conservation funders that rarely see decolonial scholarship they can support. The same body of work can be pitched to both panels, which most climate humanities projects cannot do.

02 · Fieldwork self sufficiency

One researcher can gather both the testimony and the biophysical evidence on the same visit, the oral history and the water sample, the family story and the reef survey. This lowers cost, compresses timelines, and keeps the human and the ecological record bound together from the moment of collection rather than stitched afterward.

03 · The blue carbon seam

Mangroves, seagrasses, and tidal marshes are now sold as carbon credits, and they sit on exactly the coasts where Black and Indigenous communities live. This is the sharpest live instance of Provocation II, and it requires someone who can read the marine ecology and the financial instrument together. That seam is hard to staff and native to this profile.

04 · The submerged archive

Scuba opens an archive that stays closed to most humanities programs: drowned settlements and shorelines, coral and sediment that record climate like rings in wood, and the maritime heritage of the Atlantic passage itself. The diver reaches sources the desk and the deck cannot.

05 · Credibility and a community of practice

The program's anchor geographies are coastal and marine, the Gullah Geechee Lowcountry of tidal marsh and sweetgrass, the reefs and shores of the Caribbean, the Atlantic coast of Bahia. A marine scientist is not a visitor there but a peer, and the connection to Black marine science networks supplies both credibility with reviewers and a standing, community accountable community of practice. The science is not bolted on. It is part of how the communities are met.

The work that needs both halves at once

Some of the program's most original work is simply not available to a team holding only one half of this profile. It is worth stating that plainly, because it is the clearest case for the value of the convergence.

  • Reading a garden and a reef as one archive. The provision ground on land and the cultivated water just offshore are a single system of subsistence knowledge. Only a researcher fluent in both agronomy and marine ecology can read them together.
  • Auditing a carbon market against the ecology it prices. To ask whether a blue carbon credit is sound requires knowing both the financial instrument and the seagrass meadow it claims to value. The critique in Provocation II becomes empirical rather than only theoretical.
  • Diving the evidence. Submerged sites, reef cores, and drowned coasts are primary sources that a non diver can only ever cite secondhand. Here they can be visited, surveyed, and returned to the communities that hold the surface story.
  • Designing repair, not only describing it. Conservation and restoration experience lets the reparations ecology of Provocation VII propose concrete coastal repair, tested against what actually rebuilds a marsh, rather than a purely conceptual program.
  • Speaking credibly to two review cultures. The same finding can be defended in the language of the environmental humanities and in the language of conservation science, which widens every audience the program reaches.

The risks this profile retires

A reviewer reading the prospectus will reasonably ask how a humanities lab intends to handle the science it leans on, the ecological comparison in Provocation I, the marine fieldwork in VI, the financial and biophysical literacy in II and IV. The answer is that these are not gaps to be filled later but strengths already in place. The program's points of greatest ambition coincide with the researcher's points of greatest training, which is exactly the alignment a funder hopes to find and rarely does.

The provision ground was always both a plot of earth and a relationship to water, food grown and fish taken, the marsh read like a calendar. A researcher who can stand in the field and also go beneath the water is not bringing two skills to one project. She is bringing one way of knowing the whole. The case, in a sentence
